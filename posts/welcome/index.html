The Value of Domain Experience for new Data Scientist

labs
Author

Eduardo Rivera

Published

January 15, 2026

Data Science is Evolving

One of the hottest topics in today’s world is Artificial Intelligence. It should come as no surprise for us then that the demand for data scientists in organizations, people who can understand Machine Learning and related topics, is also at an all-time high.

I was not immune to this myself, and I started looking for ways to transition into this field. I ended up enrolling into the Master’s of Data Science program at the prestigious University of British Columbia. During my wonderful time here I’ve heard a lot of concern regarding our future job prospects. This in turn made me zoom out and look at the state of the Data Science hype and its future as a field.

And I came to the conclusion that we are teaching it wrong.

The Landscape of Data Science Careers in Early 2026

After doing some research one can spot some telling statistics about the labor market in early 2026. While the Bureau of Labor Statistics projects a robust 34% growth for data science roles through 2034 [1], the reality for new entrants is much starker. Entry-level tech job postings in the US have plummeted by 67% in recent years.[2] Even more concerning, statistics suggest that up to 42% of available entry-level listings are “ghost jobs”—positions that exist on paper but are never intended to be filled.[2] Meanwhile, the demand for “specialist” roles has surged by 49%, creating a massive divide between junior generalists and senior experts.[3]

What Happened?

What’s going on? Was this not the hottest job of the century barely 3 years ago? To understand this I want to take us back to the early 2000s (because I’m old). During those days, there was a similar frenzy regarding the “dot-com” bubble, and people were trying to learn HTML and CSS left and right, the role of “webmaster” (now extinct) was THE thing to aspire to. But after the advent of new tools, new software (WordPress, Wix, the now also extinct Macromedia Dreamweaver, Macromedia Flash… I feel old just typing this) made the need to know how to code a website from scratch less and less important. The advent of generative AI ended up burying these roles for good.

The same thing is happening, with some caveats, to Data Science. This began with the “Bootcamp” phenomenon, which promised all the training needed to land a Data Science job in only 12 weeks or less. This whole sector has entirely collapsed by early 2026. Major players like Codeup, Juno College, and Kenzie Academy have ceased operations, and even industry giants like 2U have pivoted away from the bootcamp model entirely.[4][5]

The industry, flooded by a large number of brand new “Data Scientist”, naturally gravitated towards more established and prestigious training institutions, which explains the explosion of Master’s Degrees. Between 2022 and 2025 alone, the number of AI-focused master’s programs in the US jumped from 116 to 310—a staggering 167% increase.[6] This shift toward specialized fields such as Machine Learning or Artificial Intelligence reflects a market where the demand for “Generalist” Data Scientist has lowered, while job postings requiring a relevant degree have risen to 70%.[7]

What does this mean for prospective Data Scientists?

The industry is moving towards specialized Data Scientist. Gone are the days of the role of “General Data Scientist” that worked on jupyter notebooks and handed out model results for others to implement. This is no longer the only task of a Data Scientist but a minimum requirement. What companies are looking for now is professionals who can do CI/CD, have mastery over git and containerization, and a firm grasp on how to deploy the value generating models into production.[8]

Or, equally attractive, is the Data Scientist with deep domain knowledge. In particular, there are 3 main highly sought after areas where this knowledge is a big plus:

  • Finance and Fintech (Financial Data Analyst)
  • Healthcare and Biotechnology (Biomedical Data Scientist)
  • Supply Chain and Manufacturing (Industrial Data Scientist)

Of course, there are others, but the examples here highlight the rise of the “Hybrid” professional, that uses the Data Science toolkit to tackle challenges on their specific domain. For example, the HR professional who uses data science to analyze retention or bias, leveraging their knowledge of psychology or sociology. Or biologist who uses data science to analyze genetic sequences.

Where do we go from here

Here’s where I believe that we stand at a crucial juncture. Some institutions and programs might go the way of the Dodo if they don’t adapt quickly, and the consequences could be dire for the thousands and thousands of students that put their hopes on them, and for the future of the field itself.

This is why I think that the future of Data Science education requires a different focus, similar to other advanced degrees like law, complementing an already existing degree, such as biology, business, or social sciences.

Additionally, it’s important to offer students the opportunity to specialize early on in their studies, and to provide them with the tools and knowledge to do so, such as through internships or project-based learning, or through a capstone project (some of them already do this).

And for the students already in formation, it’s crucial for them to start thinking about their specialization, and how their previous experience can give them an edge. This will be our main defense against rising AI automation. In a world where AI can perform the analysis, the value shifts to the human who knows what to analyze and how to interpret the results within a specific business context.

Conclusion

In conclusion, Data Scientists won’t be the “webmasters” of the next decade, but the landscape is rapidly changing. The future belongs to those who can combine deep domain expertise with strong data science skills, and to those who can do the full path from data collection to model deployment. The “Gold Rush” is over, but for those who possess the “Context Moat” of domain expertise and the “Engineering Rigor” of a builder, the future remains exceptionally bright.
